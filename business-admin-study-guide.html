LECTURE 01

The Foundations of Business

▼
Core Definitions

Business

An entity that provides goods or services to consumers in order to earn a profit.

Revenue

All funds received from selling goods or services — before any expenses are deducted.

Profit

What remains after all business expenses are subtracted from revenue. The primary goal of a business.

Entrepreneur

An individual who takes risks to start and manage a new business, seeking profit through innovation.

Profit = Revenue − Expenses
Types of Business Output
TypeDescriptionExamples
GoodsPhysical, tangible productsiPhone, Mac, Apple Watch
ServicesIntangible offeringsBanking, hotels, airlines, hospitals
HybridSells goods AND provides servicesCar dealerships, Apple Stores
Participants & Stakeholders
  • Owners — invest capital to start and sustain the business
  • Employees — perform the operational work
  • Customers — generate demand and revenue
  • Stakeholders — any group with an interest in the business: vendors, landlords, bankers, policymakers, employees, customers. Their interests often conflict and require balancing.
Apple Case Study: Started as a $1,300 garage startup. Key success factors: innovative products, strong leadership, dedicated employees, calculated risk-taking, and perfect timing. Today it approaches a $3 trillion valuation.
Quick Review — Lecture 1

Revenue ≠ Profit
Entrepreneur = risk-taker who starts businesses
Services are intangible; goods are tangible
Stakeholders have conflicting interests
Core participants: Owners, Employees, Customers
LECTURE 02

Functional Areas & External Forces (PESTEL)

▼
The 5 Core Business Functions

Management

Planning, organizing, leading, and controlling resources to achieve goals.

Operations

Transforming labor, materials, money, and information into goods/services while ensuring quality.

Marketing

Identifying customer needs, developing products, pricing, and maintaining customer relationships.

Accounting

Measuring and communicating financial data. Financial = external users; Managerial = internal decisions.

Finance

Determining funding needs, managing investments (plant, equipment, R&D), and planning payback.

PESTEL Macro-Environment

LECTURE 03

Management & Leadership — The 4 Functions

▼
Efficiency vs. Effectiveness

Efficiency

"Doing things right" — using resources (labor, money, time, equipment) wisely with minimal waste.

Effectiveness

"Doing the right things" — achieving the desired organizational goals and results.

True Success = Efficiency + Effectiveness
The 4 Functions of Management (The Cycle)
1

Planning

Setting clear goals and determining how to achieve them. Answers: Where are we going? How do we get there? — Sets prices, schedules, budgets, and strategies.

2

Organizing

Assigning tasks, creating clear structure, allocating resources. Ensures the right people are in the right roles (who writes, who prints, who sells).

3

Leading

Motivating employees, providing guidance, building team spirit. Even perfect planning fails without a capable leader to inspire.

4

Controlling

Measuring performance vs. objectives and taking corrective action. Example: planned 100 packages, sold 80 → identify cause and fix it.

Notes-4-You Case: A student notices messy notes before exams and creates a campus note-selling service. Without a manager: quality varies, deadlines are missed, costs rise. With a manager: coordination, cost control, quality assurance, and performance motivation combine to ensure success.
Why Each Function Matters Without the Others
  • Without Planning — Organizing has no structure, Leading has no direction, Controlling has no standards
  • Without Organizing — Tasks overlap, people don't know their roles, resources are wasted
  • Without Leading — Team lacks motivation; plans don't translate to action
  • Without Controlling — No way to detect or correct problems; goals drift
Quick Review — Lecture 3
Efficiency = doing things right (resources)
Effectiveness = doing the right things (goals)
4 Functions: Plan → Organize → Lead → Control
Management is a continuous cycle
Controlling = comparing actual vs. planned results
A company can be effective but inefficient (achieves goals but wastes resources)
LECTURE 04

Strategic Planning — Purpose, Mission, Values & SWOT

▼
Why Planning Comes First
  • Planning is the foundation of all other management functions
  • Good planning gives: Direction, Focus, Clear Goals
  • Planning reduces: confusion, waste, and uncertainty
  • Planning answers: What to do, Who does it, How it's done
The 4-Step Strategic Planning Process
1

Define the Purpose

Why does the organization exist? What value does it create? — Example: Walmart's purpose was to offer lowest prices with best service.

2

Mission Statement

An official declaration of the organization's purpose. Defines purpose, guides strategic decisions, aligns employees, conveys identity. Example: Mary Kay — "Enriching women's lives around the world."

3

Core Values

Fundamental beliefs guiding employee behavior and decisions. Must be practiced, not just written. Example: Volvo's core values = Safety, Quality, Care for the Environment.

4

SWOT Analysis

Assess Strengths, Weaknesses (internal) and Opportunities, Threats (external) to make better strategic decisions.

SWOT Analysis — Internal vs. External
FactorSourceImpactExamples
StrengthsInternalAdvantagesMotivated workforce, modern technology, strong management, good location
WeaknessesInternalConstraintsPoor performance, old equipment, weak leadership, limited resources
OpportunitiesExternalPositive potentialNew law requiring bicycle helmets → demand increase for helmet makers
ThreatsExternalNegative risksTwo large sports companies entering the helmet market → more competition
After SWOT: Use strengths to exploit opportunities · Fix weaknesses · Protect against threats · Make better decisions
Quick Review — Lecture 4
Strategic plan = long-term direction (starts at top management)
Mission = Why the org exists

SWOT: S & W = Internal; O & T = External
Volvo values: Safety, Quality, Environment
Mary Kay mission: short but inspiring
Planning without SWOT = blind strategy
LECTURE 05

Goals, Tactical & Operational Plans, Crisis Management

▼
Goals vs. Objectives

Goals

Broad, long-term achievements. Set overall direction. Span several years. Example: "Achieve strong financial performance."

Objectives

Short-term, specific, measurable targets that guide daily activities. Must be: Clearly stated · Measurable · Achievable · Time-specific · Assigned to a responsible person.

Mission = Why · Goals = What (long-term) · Objectives = How & When (short-term)
Types of Organizational Goals
  • Financial Goals — Maximize profit, drive sales growth, enhance ROI
  • Operational Goals — Improve efficiency, boost productivity, ensure quality
  • Social & Relationship Goals — Employee satisfaction, community engagement, environmental responsibility
McDonald's Example: Instead of opening new restaurants, they shifted goals to improving existing ones. Measurable objectives: 3–5% annual sales growth, 6–7% income growth, improved service speed scores. Goals are dynamic — they must evolve with the business environment.
The 3-Level Planning Hierarchy
Plan LevelTime FrameFocusExample
Strategic PlanLong-term (3–5+ years)Overall direction & big picture"Build customer loyalty"
Tactical PlanMedium-term (~1 year)Departmental actions & initiatives"Improve delivery speed"
Operational PlanShort-term (weeks/months)Daily tasks & individual responsibilities"Submit notes earlier"
Contingency Planning vs. Crisis Management
AspectContingency PlanningCrisis Management
NaturePreventive / ProactiveReactive but prepared
TimingBefore an event (backup plan)During a sudden serious event
PurposePrepare for possible risksRespond to emergencies
ExamplesSupply chain disruption plan; market shift scenariosProduct recall, industrial accident, PR disaster

Wendy's — Good Crisis Response

False fingertip claim → quick investigation, transparency, $100K reward, local community offers, no overreaction → fast recovery.

BP — Poor Crisis Response

Deepwater Horizon oil spill → CEO's insensitive comments made things worse → irreparable reputation damage. Lesson: leadership communication is critical.

Modern Crisis Reality: Companies face intense social media scrutiny, increased public distrust, and complex legal risks. Managers must invest in anticipating crises, practicing response scenarios, and training emergency teams.

Wendy's = good crisis; BP = poor crisis

Strategic plan → broken into 1-year tactical plans
